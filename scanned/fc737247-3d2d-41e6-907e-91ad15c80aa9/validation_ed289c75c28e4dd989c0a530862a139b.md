### Title
`addLiquidity()` and `removeLiquidity()` bypass the `whenNotPaused` guard, allowing LP fund extraction during a paused pool state — (`metric-core/contracts/MetricOmmPool.sol`)

---

### Summary

`MetricOmmPool.swap()` is protected by the `whenNotPaused` modifier, but `addLiquidity()` and `removeLiquidity()` are not. When the pool is paused (e.g., due to an oracle incident or security event), LPs can still add and remove liquidity, bypassing the intended freeze and allowing fund extraction from a pool whose state may be compromised.

---

### Finding Description

In `MetricOmmPool`, the `pauseLevel` state variable controls whether the pool is frozen. The `_checkNotPaused()` internal function reverts when `pauseLevel != 0`, and the `whenNotPaused` modifier wraps it. [1](#0-0) [2](#0-1) 

`swap()` correctly applies this guard: [3](#0-2) 

However, `addLiquidity()` and `removeLiquidity()` carry no such modifier: [4](#0-3) [5](#0-4) 

Both functions proceed to call `LiquidityLib.addLiquidity` / `LiquidityLib.removeLiquidity` and transfer tokens out of the pool without any pause check. The extension hooks (`_beforeRemoveLiquidity`, `_afterRemoveLiquidity`) are still invoked, but the pool-level pause gate is entirely absent. [6](#0-5) 

---

### Impact Explanation

When the pool is paused (admin level 1 or protocol level 2), the intended invariant is that no user-facing state-changing operation can proceed. Because `removeLiquidity()` is unguarded:

1. **LP fund extraction during a security pause**: If the pool is paused because the oracle is returning bad prices or the pool state is corrupted, LPs can still call `removeLiquidity()` and receive token transfers. Depending on the corrupted state, they may receive more than their fair share, or drain the pool before the issue is resolved, leaving other LPs with nothing to claim once the pause is lifted.

2. **Asymmetric freeze**: Swaps are blocked (protecting traders), but LP withdrawals are not (exposing the pool to drain). This is the direct analog of the Nibbl M-01 pattern: the operations that move funds out of the pool (`removeLiquidity`, like `redeem`/`withdrawERC721`) are left open while the operations that could counteract the problem (`swap`, like `buy`/`sell`) are locked.

3. **Extension guard bypass**: Extensions such as `OracleValueStopLossExtension` or `DepositAllowlistExtension` configured on the `beforeRemoveLiquidity` hook may themselves revert on bad oracle state, but the pool-level pause is a separate, coarser safety net that is supposed to stop all operations unconditionally. Bypassing it means the pool admin's emergency freeze is ineffective for LP flows. [7](#0-6) 

---

### Likelihood Explanation

- The pool has an explicit two-level pause mechanism (`pauseLevel` 0/1/2) managed by the factory, indicating pausing is an expected operational action.
- Any pool admin or protocol operator who pauses the pool during an incident would reasonably expect all fund-moving operations to be frozen.
- Any LP can call `removeLiquidity()` directly on the pool contract without going through the router; no special privilege is required.
- The trigger is simply: pool is paused → LP calls `removeLiquidity()` → tokens are transferred out.

---

### Recommendation

Add the `whenNotPaused` modifier to both `addLiquidity()` and `removeLiquidity()`:

```solidity
function addLiquidity(...) external whenNotPaused nonReentrant(PoolActions.ADD_LIQUIDITY) ...

function removeLiquidity(...) external whenNotPaused nonReentrant(PoolActions.REMOVE_LIQUIDITY) ...
```

This mirrors the guard already applied to `swap()` and ensures the pause invariant is uniformly enforced across all user-facing state-changing operations.

---

### Proof of Concept

1. Pool is deployed with an oracle-backed price provider and an `OracleValueStopLossExtension` on the `beforeSwap` hook.
2. The oracle begins returning a manipulated price. The protocol admin calls `factory.setPause(poolAddress, 1)` to freeze the pool, setting `pauseLevel = 1`.
3. `swap()` now reverts with `PoolPaused()` — swaps are blocked as intended.
4. An LP calls `pool.removeLiquidity(owner, salt, deltas, extensionData)` directly. No `whenNotPaused` check exists; the call proceeds through `_beforeRemoveLiquidity` (extension hooks fire normally), then `LiquidityLib.removeLiquidity` executes, and tokens are transferred to the LP.
5. The LP successfully withdraws their full position from a pool that the admin intended to be completely frozen, potentially at a time when the pool's accounting is in a compromised state. [8](#0-7) [9](#0-8)
