### Title
Missing Slippage Protection on ERC4626 Deposit in `wrapVaultAsset` — (`File: core/contracts/ContractOwner.sol`)

---

### Summary

`ContractOwner.wrapVaultAsset()` calls `IERC4626Base.deposit()` to wrap a user's underlying assets into ERC4626 vault shares without any minimum-shares-received check. Because the function is callable by any unprivileged address, a sandwich attacker can manipulate the vault's share price around the deposit, causing the user's `DirectDepositV1` contract to receive fewer shares than expected. Those deflated shares are subsequently credited to the user's subaccount as collateral, resulting in a direct, measurable asset loss.

---

### Finding Description

`ContractOwner.wrapVaultAsset()` is an `external` function with no access-control modifier: [1](#0-0) 

Its body:
1. Reads `assetBalance` held by the user's `DirectDepositV1` address.
2. Checks `previewDeposit(assetBalance) != 0` — this only guards against a zero-share deposit, not against receiving fewer shares than expected.
3. Pulls the assets from `DirectDepositV1` into `ContractOwner` via `DirectDepositV1.withdraw()`.
4. Calls `IERC4626Base(tokenAddr).deposit(assetBalance, directDepositV1)` — the return value (actual shares minted) is **never captured or compared** against the `previewDeposit` estimate. [2](#0-1) 

The `IERC4626Base` interface exposes both `deposit` and `previewDeposit`, but the contract only uses `previewDeposit` as a gate check, not as a slippage bound: [3](#0-2) 

After `wrapVaultAsset` completes, the `DirectDepositV1` holds the actual (potentially manipulated) share balance. When `creditDeposit()` is subsequently called, it deposits whatever share balance is present into the protocol: [4](#0-3) 

The user's subaccount is credited with the deflated share amount, not the fair amount they paid for.

---

### Impact Explanation

A sandwich attacker inflates the ERC4626 vault's share price (e.g., by donating assets directly to the vault) before `wrapVaultAsset` executes, then deflates it after. The user's `DirectDepositV1` receives fewer shares for the same asset input. Those shares are then deposited as collateral via `creditDeposit()`, so the user's on-chain subaccount balance is permanently lower than the assets they committed. The attacker recovers their donation plus the spread. This is a direct, quantifiable collateral theft from the depositing user.

---

### Likelihood Explanation

`wrapVaultAsset` is permissionlessly callable by any address — there is no `onlyOwner` or similar guard on the function. [1](#0-0) 

An attacker only needs to observe a pending `wrapVaultAsset` call in the mempool (or front-run the user's deposit flow) and execute a standard sandwich. The attack is profitable whenever the vault's liquidity is low enough that a donation meaningfully shifts the share price. No privileged access, leaked keys, or governance capture is required.

---

### Recommendation

Capture the return value of `IERC4626Base.deposit()` and compare it against `previewDeposit(assetBalance)` (or a caller-supplied minimum). Revert if actual shares received fall below the expected minimum:

```solidity
uint256 expectedShares = IERC4626Base(tokenAddr).previewDeposit(assetBalance);
uint256 actualShares   = IERC4626Base(tokenAddr).deposit(assetBalance, directDepositV1);
require(actualShares >= expectedShares, "slippage: too few shares");
```

Alternatively, accept a `minSharesOut` parameter in `wrapVaultAsset` so the caller can enforce their own tolerance.

---

### Proof of Concept

1. User sends 1,000 USDC to their `DirectDepositV1` address for a vault-backed product.
2. Attacker observes the pending `wrapVaultAsset` call.
3. Attacker front-runs: donates 9,000 USDC directly to the ERC4626 vault, inflating the share price 10×.
4. `wrapVaultAsset` executes: `deposit(1000 USDC, directDepositV1)` mints ~100 shares instead of ~1,000.
5. Attacker back-runs: redeems their own shares at the inflated price, recovering their 9,000 USDC plus profit from the spread.
6. `creditDeposit()` is called: the 100 shares are deposited into the protocol.
7. User's subaccount is credited with collateral worth ~100 USDC instead of ~1,000 USDC — a 90% loss.
