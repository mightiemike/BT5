### Title
Self-Liquidation Bypass via Alternative Address Captures Liquidation Discount — (`core/contracts/ClearinghouseLiq.sol`)

---

### Summary

`ClearinghouseLiq.liquidateSubaccountImpl` guards against self-liquidation with a single `bytes32` equality check (`txn.sender != txn.liquidatee`). Because a Nado subaccount is `address (20 bytes) || subaccountName (12 bytes)`, a user who controls two EOAs can liquidate their own unhealthy subaccount from a second address, bypassing the restriction entirely and capturing the liquidation discount that is intended to reward third-party liquidators.

---

### Finding Description

`liquidateSubaccountImpl` enforces:

```solidity
require(txn.sender != txn.liquidatee, ERR_UNAUTHORIZED);
``` [1](#0-0) 

This compares the full 32-byte subaccount identifiers. Two subaccounts derived from different EOAs are always unequal, so the check passes even when the same economic actor controls both sides of the liquidation.

The `_handleLiquidationPayment` function computes a liquidation price below oracle price and splits the spread:

```solidity
v.liquidationFees = (v.oraclePriceX18 - v.liquidationPriceX18)
    .mul(LIQUIDATION_FEE_FRACTION)
    .mul(txn.amount);
``` [2](#0-1) 

`LIQUIDATION_FEE_FRACTION = 50%` goes to the insurance fund; the remaining 50% of the spread is the liquidator's profit — the assets are acquired at `liquidationPrice` but are worth `oraclePrice`. [3](#0-2) 

---

### Impact Explanation

**Concrete asset delta per self-liquidation:**

| Party | Change |
|---|---|
| Subaccount A (liquidatee, user-controlled) | Loses `amount` of asset; gains `liquidationPrice × amount` in quote |
| Subaccount B (liquidator, user-controlled) | Gains `amount` of asset worth `oraclePrice × amount`; loses `(liquidationPrice + liquidationFees/amount) × amount` in quote |
| Insurance fund | Gains `liquidationFees` |

**User net (A + B combined):**
- Asset position: flat (A loses, B gains)
- Quote: `−liquidationFees` (only the insurance cut)

**Third-party liquidation net for the same user:**
- Quote: `−liquidationFees − (oraclePrice − liquidationPrice) × amount × (1 − LIQUIDATION_FEE_FRACTION)`

The user saves `(oraclePrice − liquidationPrice) × amount × 0.5` — the 50% spread that would otherwise flow to an independent liquidator. On a $1 M position with a 5% liquidation discount, this is **$25,000 per liquidation event** captured by the attacker rather than a third party.

Secondary impact: the liquidation incentive mechanism is undermined. If large traders routinely self-liquidate, third-party liquidators have reduced incentive to monitor and liquidate positions, degrading protocol solvency guarantees.

---

### Likelihood Explanation

- Any user can generate a second EOA at zero cost.
- The user only needs to fund subaccount B sufficiently to cover the liquidation payment.
- The user controls the timing of the liquidation (e.g., submitting it the moment their position crosses the maintenance threshold), minimising slippage and maximising the captured discount.
- No privileged access, governance action, or external dependency is required.

Likelihood: **Medium** (requires deliberate setup but is trivially executable by any sophisticated trader).

---

### Recommendation

Replace the subaccount-level equality check with an address-level check so that no two subaccounts sharing the same underlying EOA can be on opposite sides of a liquidation:

```solidity
require(
    address(uint160(bytes20(txn.sender))) !=
        address(uint160(bytes20(txn.liquidatee))),
    ERR_UNAUTHORIZED
);
``` [1](#0-0) 

---

### Proof of Concept

1. **Setup**: Attacker controls `address1` and `address2`.
2. Deposit collateral into `subaccountA = bytes32(abi.encodePacked(address1, "default\x00\x00\x00\x00\x00"))`.
3. Open a large leveraged perp position in `subaccountA` until it is under maintenance margin.
4. Fund `subaccountB = bytes32(abi.encodePacked(address2, "default\x00\x00\x00\x00\x00"))` with enough quote to cover the liquidation payment.
5. Submit a sequencer-routed `LiquidateSubaccount` transaction:
   - `sender = subaccountB`
   - `liquidatee = subaccountA`
   - `productId` = the perp product
   - `amount` = full liquidatable amount
6. `EndpointTx.processTransactionImpl` validates the signature against `subaccountB` and charges `LIQUIDATION_FEE = $1` to `subaccountB`. [4](#0-3) 
7. `liquidateSubaccountImpl` checks `subaccountB != subaccountA` — **passes** because the `bytes32` values differ. [1](#0-0) 
8. `_handleLiquidationPayment` transfers the perp position to `subaccountB` at `liquidationPrice`, crediting `liquidationFees` to insurance. [5](#0-4) 
9. `subaccountB` now holds assets worth `oraclePrice × amount` having paid only `liquidationPrice × amount + liquidationFees`. The attacker captures `(oraclePrice − liquidationPrice) × amount × 0.5` that would otherwise have gone to an independent liquidator.
