### Title
Authz `GenericAuthorization` for `MsgTransfer` Allows Grantee to Redirect Granter's NFT to Arbitrary Receiver via IBC — (`x/nft-transfer/keeper/msg_server.go`, `x/nft-transfer/keeper/packet.go`)

---

### Summary

A grantee holding a `GenericAuthorization` for `/chainmain.nft_transfer.v1.Msg/Transfer` can call `MsgExec([MsgTransfer{Sender: granter, Receiver: attacker_on_dst, ...}])` to permanently transfer the granter's NFT to an attacker-controlled address on the destination chain, without the granter ever signing the IBC transfer.

---

### Finding Description

The exploit path is fully traceable through production code:

**Step 1 — Authz signer check passes.**

`MsgTransfer.GetSigners()` derives the required signer from `msg.Sender`: [1](#0-0) 

When the grantee submits `MsgExec([MsgTransfer{Sender: granter, Receiver: attacker, ...}])`, the authz module calls `GetSigners()` on the inner message, receives `[granter]`, finds the grantee holds a `GenericAuthorization` from the granter for this message type, and proceeds to execute the inner message. The grantee controls the `Receiver` field freely — `GenericAuthorization` imposes no field-level restrictions.

**Step 2 — `Transfer` handler trusts `msg.Sender` without verifying the actual tx signer.** [2](#0-1) 

`sender` is derived directly from `msg.Sender` (the granter's address). `msg.Receiver` (the attacker's address) is passed through unchanged. There is no check that the actual transaction signer equals `msg.Sender`.

**Step 3 — Owner check in `createOutgoingPacket` passes because `sender` = granter = NFT owner.** [3](#0-2) 

`sender` is the granter, who is the legitimate owner. The check `sender.Equals(owner)` succeeds. The NFT is then escrowed or burned: [4](#0-3) 

**Step 4 — Packet is sent with `receiver` = attacker.** [5](#0-4) 

The `receiver` field in the packet data is the attacker's address on the destination chain. On acknowledgement, the destination chain mints or unescrows the NFT to the attacker.

---

### Impact Explanation

The granter's NFT is permanently and irrecoverably moved to the attacker on the destination chain. The source chain escrows or burns the NFT. There is no refund path unless the IBC packet times out — but the attacker can choose a timeout far in the future, and once the packet is acknowledged, the NFT is gone. This is a direct, permanent asset loss for the granter.

---

### Likelihood Explanation

Any user who grants a `GenericAuthorization` for `MsgTransfer` (e.g., to a bot, a dApp, or a trusted relayer) is immediately vulnerable. The grant is a standard Cosmos SDK authz operation. The grantee needs no special privileges beyond the grant itself. The IBC channel must exist, which is a normal operational precondition.

---

### Recommendation

The `Transfer` handler must verify that the actual transaction signer matches `msg.Sender`. Retrieve the signer from the SDK context (e.g., via `sdk.UnwrapSDKContext(goCtx).TxBytes()` or by comparing against the authz-resolved signer) and reject any message where `msg.Sender` does not equal the resolved signer. Alternatively, implement a custom `Authorization` type (instead of relying on `GenericAuthorization`) that restricts the `Receiver` field to a pre-approved set of addresses, similar to how `ibc-go`'s `TransferAuthorization` restricts fungible token transfers.
